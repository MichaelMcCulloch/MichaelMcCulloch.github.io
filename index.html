Research Themes

From Dissipative Systems to Free Energy

Hurricanes, living cells, candle flames, stars, brains — all of these are examples of Prigogine’s dissipative systems (Prigogine, 1977). These systems, operating at non‑equilibrium steady state, maintain their Markov blanket — their structure — by exporting entropy (Pearl, 1988; Friston, 2019). Hurricanes maintain their structure by exporting wind, cells by exporting heat, candle flames by exporting combustion products, stars by exporting radiation, and brains by exporting surprise, in the Bayesian sense (Nicolis & Prigogine, 1977; Friston, 2019; Chirumbolo & Vella, 2024).

From Prigogine, systems are organized into non‑equilibrium steady state (NESS) via the continuous flow of energy through them (Prigogine, 1977; Schrödinger, 1944). Once the energy source is removed, the dissipative structure collapses into thermodynamic equilibrium with its environment.

From Friston, systems maintain their Markov blanket — the separation between internal and external states — by continuously acting to minimize surprisal. They do this by importing low entropy and exporting high entropy. Systems that fail to maintain the Markov blanket dissolve into equilibrium with their environment (Friston, 2010, 2019; Friston et al., 2023). This maintenance of the Markov blanket is what we refer to as self‑evidencing.

The Gap: From Principle to Mechanism

While FEP provides a theoretical account, and NESS a descriptive one, the path from what to how has historically been unclear. It remained to be seen, for instance, how a physical system composed only of locally interacting components could cooperate to meet a global imperative.

The challenge is one of credit assignment: determining causal responsibility with enough precision to steer the network toward a goal. This decomposes into spatial, temporal, and structural credit assignment.

  • Spatial: which parameter is responsible for error?
  • Temporal: when did a parameter’s firing matter, given delayed feedback?
  • Structural: which parameters should exist at all?

Classical deep learning deals well with spatial and temporal credit assignment via backpropagation and BPTT. Structural credit assignment has largely been outsourced to architecture search and pruning heuristics.

Autonomous Assembly of Neural Structure (AANS)
A dissipative implementation of the Free Energy Principle
Active Project

Abstract

By contrast to biological systems, deep learning operates on a fundamentally unphysical paradigm of backpropagation, almost without exception. In this work I present a constructive proof that the Free Energy Principle can be realized via exact local credit assignment in the form of a dissipative system that minimizes variational free energy.

The primary contribution is a trophic field map that solves the problem of structural credit assignment — which connections should even be present in the network. This mechanism is informed by eligibility traces (Sutton, 1984, 1988) and weight transport, although the latter can be substituted with feedback alignment (Lillicrap et al., 2016). This architecture unifies Prigogine’s dissipative systems with Friston’s variational free energy by demonstrating they can be told as one story.

From Theory to Architecture

Classical solutions to credit assignment require non‑local information and violate the physical constraints of biological systems. Backpropagation requires symmetric feedback connections. BPTT requires storing complete gradient trajectories (Werbos, 1990). Architecture search requires global fitness signals or exhaustive enumeration. None of these mechanisms are consistent with local, online, and continual learning.

AANS differs by proposing a system that performs continuous, online learning without phased learning, and introduces a trophic field model for exact, gradient‑based structural credit assignment that allows the network architecture itself to be inferred as part of the free energy minimization process.

Empirical Behavior

The resulting system behaves less like a static model and more like a physical process:

  • Lunar lander in < 30 episodes on average, no replay buffers or rollouts.
  • >97% Pearson correlation with eligibility propagation for structural credit assignment.
  • Online, lifelong, experiential learning with 0% catastrophic forgetting in the tested regimes.
  • Sub‑50 ms latency at full GPU saturation, with no settling period.

LLMs, Compilers, and Tools

Is the LLM Really the Next Compiler?

A walk from punch cards → assemblers → compilers → virtual machines → large language models, framing LLMs as the latest abstraction over “how” we program rather than an end to programming. Assemblers didn’t kill programming. Compilers didn’t kill programming. Interpreters didn’t kill programming. LLMs subsume rote skill and widen the field of tooling options; they don’t erase discernment.

Silicon vs. Biology

The biological brain developed under strict energy constraints. It is optimized for efficiency and resilience to noise, not for raw throughput or precision. Silicon is not a better map of the biological territory of intelligence — it is a different territory entirely: tensor cores, CUDA, flash NAND, fibre interlinks, and a distributed grid producing unimaginable power.

The future of AI is not in building a more efficient brain, but in building something that is not a brain at all: a computational entity that leverages speed, precision, and memory on a scale that biology could never dream of, consuming energy on a commensurate scale. Silicon is the enabling technology for a higher order of intelligence, not a teleological higher form.

The Internet as a Dissipative System

The early internet (v1) was a world of personal sites, Newgrounds, Neopets, and YTMND — just people being people online. Internet v2 consolidated into a handful of platforms and traffic monopolies. Internet v3 is closer to a country of ghost towns. The people left; the bots stayed. LLMs now occupy the empty storefronts where humans used to live.

Risk, AGI, and “If Anyone Builds It, Everyone Dies”

AI risk is less about science fiction and more about game theory plus human hubris. If all 8 billion people on Earth played chess against Stockfish, all 8 billion would lose. What experts once thought impossible for computers (chess, Go, Stratego, Dota) became trivial once the enabling technology landed. “AI will never X” has a poor historical record.

The control problem is fundamentally a math problem. You can’t fight what you don’t understand. Humanity’s failure to properly grapple with climate change is a preview of what happens when complicated physical processes meet limited collective cognition.

First Principles

First Principles Over Rote

I do not subscribe to rote memorization. As a calculus teacher once told me: if you can’t derive it from scratch, you don’t know the material. This applies as much to CUDA kernels and cloud architectures as it does to thermodynamics and inference.

Depth × Breadth = Volume

I see every problem as a unique opportunity for a first principles solution, because no two problems are alike and each deserves the full breadth of knowledge I can bring to bear. The most value is brought to bear by maximizing the area under that curve, not by specializing deep or generalizing wide alone.

Tools as Abstractions, Not Identity

I am not a Java program. Sometimes I choose to write them, but that’s a tool choice, the same way choosing a deque or a stack is a tool choice. The large language model subsumes the tools, but not the use of them. The skill in programming is the correct use — taste and discernment — of the correct tool.

Entropy as Constraint

Whether building ML infrastructure or neuromorphic‑style systems, I treat entropy as a design constraint. Minimal surfaces, minimal hair. The fewer ways a system can fail, the longer it can stand against the tide.
